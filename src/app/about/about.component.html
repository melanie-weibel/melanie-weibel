The beginning of an era

My journey into programming started in the last year of highschool by yet another random spontaneous interest on mine. Basically, I woke up one day thinking : "What if I learnt how to program stuff?"

So here I went, using the power of internet. After a few reads, I decided that C langages was the basics of the basic, and I set my sight on it. Started days of trials and errors, bugs hunt and murder as I walked thouroulessly through a tutoriel on OpenClassroom (formely Le site du Zero). Nothing was never working at the first try, but that was surprinsingly fun. I found myself enjoying the hardest parts, beating problems with my never give up mentality, and feeling super rewarded when things finally came together.

Then, I woke up another morning with another random spontaneous need to learn a new thing, and it became a fond memory, a skill waiting its time to be useful again.

The cover of the first widely avaible book on the C language, published in 1978. Not where I learnt it, thought.
I still don't know WHY someone thought that was a great decision, but, hey, I'll take it.

It came back into relevency two years later, at a pretty unexpected time. I always say I became a programmer because

During highschool, as I was strggling to decide what I wanted to do of my life, in a scientific cursus because that is the one which (saldy) leave the most options open in France, I figured that rather thinking about a domain, I would seek what I needed to be professsionnally happy. Tree points stood out : working in a team, having a (?) and being able to use my creativness.

And... that lead kind of nowhere, because it can be applied to tons of different jobs. Listening to family recomandation, I went into preparatory classes for prestigous engineeer school. That was a nice plan, except I end up disliking both the cursus and where it was going : I realised I didn't wanted to be a engineer. My parents wouldn't let my waste the one year and a half I did, so I went for a brige with university. I had the choice between engeneerign science, maths, physics, and - weirdly - informatic. I never understood why it was there, as we only did some Python, taught by the math teacher, so it was mostly match algorithm, but that was the only thing I wasn't fed off at the time, so I went for it.

When things went in motion

It was the middle of the school year, but the start of a new semester, with all that comes along it : new courses, new teachers but same schoolmates - except for me. The vert first day, I learnt that the subjet of one my courses was to programm the othello game in C langage. Not to learn it, because all the other student learned it earlier in the formation. Weird bridge, I told you.

Fortunatly, when I learn about a subject, I learn thourougly and durably, so my little expedition into programmation shone. I actually had the level, and I never felt like I had to get up(?) to theirs. What followed was one of the greatest moments(?) of my life, because I realised that I simply *loved* programming. It was more than this, actually : it fit me perfectly. My never-give up attitude? Perfect for hunting bugs relentlessy. My drive to make things correctly? A nice archtecture and clean code. My creativity? Tons of ideas and problem solving capacity. My desire for recogntion(?) ? The feeling of accomplisment when things build from my own hands finally work. My curiosity ? Perfect for exploring new options and technologies.

From the point of vue of my professionnaly accomplishment(?), it was really good too : team projects and a ever evolving environment. The only part that was lacking was the creativity aspect. There wasn't many liberties to take when doing exercices or even the othello in C language project. I took some options trying to fix that, like natural langage (the goal was to learn how translation program works with sequecing sentences) and web developpement. For the first course, we had a small project to do, and I ended up doing a search motor of french words in japanase songs. The difficulty came to recognise the words in the japanase text because they have no spaces between words and almost no pontuaction. (img japanse text)

As for the web development, I quickly fell in love with it. The visual and imediate result was very appealing to me, as someone with an art sense and a good visual memory. In the first course, we did a lot of practical an quick exercise, but in the end we got to make a 2D game with a little phisics in a local javascript page (with a bit of css and hmtl, of course). I went for a platform game, but my ambitions were too high and I lost of lot of time trying to make menus and graphics rather than the actual game. I learnt the hard way the terrible pain of hitboxes.

The next year, the whole course was based around making a real website, as we were introduced to PHP and SQL. WampServer, Jmerise and Phpmyadmin (my sql) would go on to be useful tools, and the website in question - a pokemon fansite made by three of us - is still around and up online, thought it's not my proudest realisation. Mainly, because for some reason, we decided to make the whole visual by hand with basic css only, so it doesn't look very modern, to say the least. I pretty happy with the whole database request system, on the other hand. (image piliscope.fr) -
